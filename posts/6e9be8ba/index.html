Summary of Lesson The Discrete Fourier Transform

In this lesson, we have studied in greater details the discrete Fourier transform (DFT), the Fourier analysis tool for finite-length signals.

The DFT is just a simple change of basis. The analysis formula (moving from the time to the frequency domain) is given by the inner product of the signal with the Fourier basis vector,

\[ \begin{align} X[k] &= \langle \mathbf{w}^{(k)}, \mathbf{x} \rangle \\ &= \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] e^{-j \frac{2\pi}{N} nk} \end{align}. \]

The synthesis formula (moving from the frequency to the time domain) is a linear combination of the basis functions, scaled by the coefficients we found in the analysis formula,

\[ \begin{align} x[n] &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X[k]\mathbf{w}^{(k)} \\ &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X[k] e^{j \frac{2\pi}{N} nk} \end{align} \]

In the most general case, the DFT coefficients \(X[k]\) are complex numbers and thus have a real and imaginary part. In order to interpret these coefficients, it is convenient to represent their magnitude \(|X[k]|\) and phase \(X[k]\), wrapped between \(-\) and \(\). In a DFT plot, frequencies between \(0\) and \(N/2\) (resp. \(N/2\) and \(N-1\) correspond to digital frequencies smaller than \(\) (resp. larger than \(\)), in other words counterclockwise rotations (resp. clockwise rotations). Frequencies close to \(0\) and \(N-1\) corresponds to the low frequencies and those close to \(N/2\) to the high frequencies.

Finally, the DFT of a real signal is symmetric in magnitude, mathematically

\(|X[k]| = |X[N-k]|, k = 1, …, .\)
